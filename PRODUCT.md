# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Hiring managers and technical recruiters at product companies. They're evaluating dozens of candidates, skimming fast, and looking for a signal that this person is different. The goal is not to inform — it's to make them confident enough to book a call. Competence and personality in equal measure; neither alone is enough.

## Product Purpose

Personal portfolio for Axner Hägglund, a frontend developer based in Göteborg. The site exists to convert a brief visit into an interview. Success looks like: the person closes the tab wanting to reach out, not just "having seen it."

The site itself is the primary argument for Axner's skills — if it's forgettable, the case is lost before a single project is read.

## Brand Personality

Quiet confidence. Shows rather than tells. No bios that list adjectives. No skills progress bars at 92%. Every design decision earns its place. The copy voice is dry, direct, a little wry — the work of someone who arrived at this deliberately and knows why.

Three words: deliberate, dry, earned.

## Anti-references

- Generic dev portfolio template: hero + avatar, skills grid, timeline résumé, identical card rows — could belong to anyone.
- Anything that tries too hard to prove craft: excessive cursor effects, WebGL overload, animations that block reading.
- Safe and corporate: no personality, no risk, forgettable.

## Design Principles

1. **The site is the proof.** Don't describe the skills — demonstrate them in the interface itself.
2. **Earned surprise.** Moments of delight should be quiet and discovered, never announced.
3. **Restraint as confidence.** Removing an element is harder than adding one. Blank space is not emptiness.
4. **Voice over volume.** The copy does real work. One sentence that's true beats three that are filler.
5. **Context before content.** Each page should feel like it belongs in the same world, not a different template.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- `prefers-reduced-motion` is already respected throughout — maintain this.
- WCAG AA minimum. Colour contrast must pass on both dark (home) and light (about) surfaces.
- Screen reader consideration for animated/typewriter elements — existing `aria-hidden` pattern should be preserved and extended.
